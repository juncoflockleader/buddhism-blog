# Majjhima Nikāya Readings — Part I

*This is the first entry in a series working through the Majjhima Nikāya in order. The plan is to read each sutta closely, compare available translations, and note points of difficulty or interest.*

---

## MN 1 · Mūlapariyāya Sutta — The Root of All Things

**PTS reference:** M i 1–6
**Translations consulted:** Ñāṇamoli/Bodhi (Wisdom, 1995); Bhikkhu Sujato (SuttaCentral, 2018); Thanissaro Bhikkhu (ATI)

The collection opens with one of its most difficult texts. The Mūlapariyāya is frustrating to first-time readers because it appears repetitive to the point of numbness — the same list of twenty-four objects (earth, water, fire, air, beings, gods, Pajāpati, Brahmā … all the way to Nibbāna) iterated four times, for four types of persons.

### The Fourfold Schema

The sutta distinguishes how four types of person cognize the same objects:

| Person | Mode of cognition |
|---|---|
| Untrained worldling | *maññati* — conceives/proliferates |
| Trainee (*sekha*) | Directly knows (*abhijānāti*) but still conceives |
| Arahant | Directly knows without conceiving |
| Tathāgata | Directly knows; the deepest form of knowing |

The key verb is *maññati*, from *mañña* (conceit, measurement). The untrained mind does not merely perceive earth — it *conceives* earth, meaning it elaborates, identifies with, and measures itself against it: "Earth is mine; I am earth; earth is myself."

### The Problem of Translation

The fourfold repetition is not accidental padding. Each pass through the list introduces a subtle shift: the arahant's mode of knowing is described with an additional negative particle (*na maññati* — does not conceive), while the Tathāgata's mode is characterised further still.

Ñāṇamoli/Bodhi render *maññati* as "conceives of," which captures the constructive/projective sense. Sujato uses "place" (he places himself in relation to earth), which is more idiomatic but loses the conceit connotation. Thanissaro keeps "assumes," which is defensible.

### Initial Notes

1. The sutta is addressed to monks (*bhikkhave*), not to lay practitioners — the stakes are high and the expected baseline of practice is significant.
2. The twenty-four objects include *nibbāna* itself: even liberation, if "conceived of," becomes a new basis for conceit. This is a remarkable move — the antidote can itself become poison.
3. The Commentary (*MA*) reads this as the root condition (*mūla*) for *all* views (*diṭṭhi*). That is ambitious but plausible given the structure of the sutta.
