# Dhammapada Verses 1–2: The Twin Verses

## Pāli Text

**Verse 1**

> Manopubbaṅgamā dhammā,
> manoseṭṭhā manomayā;
> Manasā ce paduṭṭhena,
> bhāsati vā karoti vā,
> Tato naṃ dukkhamanveti,
> cakkaṃva vahato padaṃ.

**Verse 2**

> Manopubbaṅgamā dhammā,
> manoseṭṭhā manomayā;
> Manasā ce pasannena,
> bhāsati vā karoti vā,
> Tato naṃ sukhamanveti,
> chāyāva anapāyinī.

---

## Translation

**Verse 1**
Mind is the forerunner of all actions.
Mind is chief; mind-made are they.
If one speaks or acts with a defiled mind,
suffering follows as the wheel follows the hoof of an ox.

**Verse 2**
Mind is the forerunner of all actions.
Mind is chief; mind-made are they.
If one speaks or acts with a serene mind,
happiness follows like a shadow that never departs.

---

## Annotation

**manopubbaṅgamā** — Literally "mind-preceded" or "mind-gone-before." The image is of mind as the lead ox in a team, or the vanguard of an army. Renderings like "mind-forerun" (Buddharakkhita) preserve the compound's forcefulness; I prefer "forerunner of all actions" to make the syntax clear in English.

**dhammā** — In this context, *dhammā* most naturally means "phenomena" or "mental-physical events" (all the constituents of experience), not *the* Dhamma (the teaching). Several translators render it "mental factors," which narrows the scope unnecessarily.

**manomayā** — "Made of mind" or "mind-fashioned." This is a strong ontological claim: experience is not merely *preceded* by mind but *constituted* by it.

**paduṭṭhena / pasannena** — "Defiled" and "serene/clear" are the standard renderings. *Paduṭṭha* has connotations of corruption and muddiness (like water stirred up), while *pasanna* suggests clarity and settling (like water that has cleared). The contrast is hydraulic.

**cakkaṃva vahato padaṃ** — "As the wheel [follows] the foot of the one who draws [the cart]." The wheel is the wheel of the cart, not the Dhamma-wheel. The image stresses inexorability: suffering does not merely sometimes follow a defiled action — it *must*, as the wheel must follow the hoof.

**chāyāva anapāyinī** — "Like a shadow that does not depart." The shadow here is your own shadow — it cannot leave you. The choice of shadow as the image for *happiness* is unusual: shadows are normally associated with coolness and transience, not joy. Perhaps the point is constancy rather than intensity.
