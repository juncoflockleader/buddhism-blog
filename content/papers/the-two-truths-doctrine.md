# The Two Truths Doctrine in Early Buddhism

## Abstract

The distinction between conventional truth (*sammuti-sacca*) and ultimate truth (*paramattha-sacca*) is central to Theravāda Abhidhamma and has exerted enormous influence on Buddhist philosophy across traditions. This paper examines the origins of the distinction in the Pāli Abhidhamma Piṭaka, its elaboration in Buddhaghosa's Visuddhimagga and Atthasālinī, and the philosophical difficulties the doctrine generates.

---

## 1. The Distinction in the Abhidhamma

The term *paramattha* ("ultimate" or "highest meaning") appears in the Dhammasaṅgaṇī, the first book of the Abhidhamma Piṭaka, in the compound *paramattha-vacana* — "expression in the ultimate sense." The Abhidhamma uses this register to describe phenomena (*dhammā*) as they are classified in the Abhidhamma's own analytical vocabulary: cittas (mind-moments), cetasikas (mental factors), rūpa (materiality), and Nibbāna.

Conventional truth, by contrast, is the language of everyday experience: persons, tables, rivers, "self." These are useful fictions — practical conventions (*sammuti*) that do not carve nature at its joints.

## 2. Buddhaghosa's Formulation

Buddhaghosa's Atthasālinī (commentary on the Dhammasaṅgaṇī) gives the canonical Theravāda account:

> "Ultimate truth is the truth of what is real in the highest sense (*paramatthasacca*). Conventional truth is the truth that holds by common consent (*sammutisacca*) — it is true insofar as it functions, but does not describe what ultimately exists."

This creates a neat ontological hierarchy: only Abhidhamma dhammas (plus Nibbāna) are ultimately real; everything else is a construct.

## 3. Philosophical Problems

### 3.1 The Grounding Problem

If persons are conventional constructs, what grounds the conventional? On the Abhidhamma picture, a "person" is a conceptual superimposition on a stream of cittas and rūpas. But this analysis presupposes that cittas and rūpas are *more real* than persons — a claim that requires defence.

One response: dhammas are *real* in the sense that they resist further analysis. A citta cannot be decomposed into more fundamental constituents in the way that a "person" can. This is an interesting criterion of reality, but it is not obviously the only defensible one.

### 3.2 The Status of Nibbāna

Nibbāna is classified as an *ultimate* (paramattha) alongside cittas, cetasikas, and rūpa. But Nibbāna is defined negatively — it is the cessation of craving, the unconditioned. How can an unconditioned, contentless "thing" be real in the same sense as a mind-moment?

Buddhaghosa addresses this by insisting that Nibbāna is a *dhamma* — an object that can be cognized by a mind-moment (*nibbāna-dhamma*). But this risks turning Nibbāna into a kind of object among objects, which sits uneasily with the soteriological claim that realization of Nibbāna involves the ending of all subject-object structuring.

## 4. Conclusion

The two-truths doctrine is a powerful analytical tool, but its Abhidhamma formulation generates genuine metaphysical tension. The most defensible reading may be pragmatic: conventional and ultimate are not two descriptions of different *things*, but two registers of description appropriate to different *purposes* — narrative and ethical purposes on one side, meditative and analytical purposes on the other. Whether the Abhidhamma itself supports this reading remains an open question.
