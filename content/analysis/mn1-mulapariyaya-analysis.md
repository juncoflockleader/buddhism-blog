# MN 1 Mūlapariyāya: Structural Analysis

**Pāli title:** Mūlapariyāya Sutta
**Location:** MN i 1 (Majjhima Nikāya, first sutta)
**Topic:** The root process (*mūla-pariyāya*) underlying all views, conceits, and proliferations

---

## 1. Overview of the Fourfold Schema

The sutta presents a typology of cognition across four types of persons — worldling, trainee, arahant, and Tathāgata — applied to twenty-four objects. The structure for each person is:

1. Perceives X as X
2. Conceives X [as X / in X / from X / as "mine"]
3. Delights in X

The four persons differ in how much of steps 2 and 3 they enact.

### 1.1 The Twenty-Four Objects

The objects fall into natural groupings:

| Group | Objects |
|---|---|
| Four elements | Earth, water, fire, air |
| Aggregated beings | Beings, gods, Pajāpati, Brahmā |
| Brahmic realms | Ābhassarā, Subhakiṇhā, Vehapphalā gods, Abhibhū |
| Formless attainments | Infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, neither-perception-nor-non-perception |
| Modes of sense cognition | Seen, heard, sensed, cognized |
| Metaphysical categories | Unity, diversity, all |
| Liberation | Nibbāna |

The list moves from the gross to the subtle, ending with Nibbāna itself.

---

## 2. The Key Verb: *Maññati*

Everything turns on *maññati* (conceives, measures, proliferates). The root *man-* underlies both *maññati* and *māna* (conceit). The untrained person:

1. **Perceives** (*sañjānāti*) earth as earth — bare perception, which itself is not the problem.
2. **Conceives** (*maññati*) earth — elaborates it through the three modes of identification (*asmimāna*): "earth is mine," "I am earth," "earth is my self."
3. **Delights** (*abhinandati*) in earth — a volitional, affective endorsement of the conception.

Steps 2 and 3 are the problem. The sutta's claim is that *all* twenty-four objects — including the subtlest attainments and Nibbāna — can become sites of conceiving if the root process is not seen through.

---

## 3. Comparison Across the Four Persons

| Person | Perceives | Conceives | Delights |
|---|---|---|---|
| Worldling | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Trainee (*sekha*) | ✓ | ✓ (reduced) | diminished |
| Arahant | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Tathāgata | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ (+ explicit grounding) |

The trainee is an interesting intermediate case: *abhijānāti* (direct knowing) is present, but *maññati* is not yet fully abandoned. This maps onto the Abhidhamma's account of the path stages: the trainee has attenuated, but not uprooted, the conceit-cetasika.

---

## 4. The Arahant's Mode in Abhidhamma Terms

In Abhidhamma terms, the untrained cognition involves *lobha* (greed) and *māna* (conceit) cetasikas. These are the unwholesome factors that perform the "conceiving." The arahant's cognition has *uprooted* these cetasikas permanently: there is no mental factor present to perform the conceiving.

This is a significant structural claim: the difference between worldling and arahant is not a matter of degree (less conceiving) but of kind (presence or absence of specific cetasikas as root factors).

---

## 5. The Tathāgata's Mode

The final iteration, describing the Tathāgata's cognition, adds an explicit grounding:

> "He has directly known earth as earth. Without conceiving earth, without conceiving in earth, without conceiving from earth, without conceiving 'earth is mine,' he does not delight in earth. **Why is that? Because delight is the root of suffering.**" (MN 1, adapted)

The formula *nandī dukkhassa mūlan ti* ("delight is the root of suffering") frames the entire analysis. Conceiving arises when cognition is infected by *nandī*, and it is this infection — not perception per se — that is the root.

---

## 6. Open Questions

1. **What is the ontological status of "direct knowing" (*abhijānāti*)?** The sutta contrasts it with *maññati* but does not define it. The Commentary reads it as Abhidhamma-style analytical insight; a phenomenological reading would emphasize bare noticing without elaboration.

2. **Does the schema apply only to the listed twenty-four objects, or is it meant to be exhaustive?** The Commentary claims exhaustiveness — the twenty-four cover all possible bases of conceiving.

3. **Why does the sutta end without comment from the monks?** The final line notes that the monks, recently ordained, did not rejoice in the teaching. This is unique in the Nikāyas. Ñāṇananda (*Concept and Reality*, 1971) reads this as deliberate: the sutta is the most radical anti-conceptual statement in the canon and is not meant to be immediately welcome.
